None of this writing out all the steps to get your answer, that's what built-in methods are like. I turned to trusty Google. One of the things I didn't think about when using Google is that there is a lot of outdated information on there. I didn't want to look at information from 5 years ago as that was most likely irrelevant now and would just confused me more, so I shortened the time frame in my search results to anything from the last year. Now I was getting somewhere. So I figured out there were actually more steps required than what I initially thought. By this stage my brain was all full from reading all the information so I took a quiet 5 minutes away from my computer to just clear my mind. I needed to talk this out so I wrote down my thinking and what steps I thought I needed to take and called my sister.

eureka! you found her!

I started walking her through my thinking, she asked me questions. Why are you doing that? What does Google say about this? How would you search that? We broke it down into smaller steps. Step one: Do I have what I need in order to be able to solve this? No, I do not. Great! What do I need in order to be able to solve it? After going back to the relevant Google tab I solved step one. Step Two: Now I've got what I need to be able to solve this problem, what is the problem I need to solve? Identify the problem. Great, now the problem is identified. Now how do I solve it? Step Three: I know what I need to be able to solve it, so how do I use it? This step was a bit of trial and error. I referred to my relevant Google tabs and started writing out what I needed to do. Each time I thought "I've got it" I would console.log it (will explain more about this later) and reflect on what was actually being shown. Each time I did a console.log I was one step closer to getting it correct. By seeing it in front of me I was able to see what had changed and identify what I needed to do. Eventually I had worked my way to an answer and got the ever desired PASS! Once I had got the pass she asked me to talk her through what I'd done. What does this do? Why did you do this? By reflecting on the work I'd done I was able to make sense of it more in a complete sense, as opposed to the smaller steps I'd taken.

You got Problem Solving techniques.

pseudocode.

Pseudocode is where you write out the steps of your code in an informal way, by not actually writing out the code but writing what it should do. I am somewhat comfortable with this, but it's usually a step in my problem solving process. First I really need to understand what the problem is, when I can move on to pseudocode. Once I write that down it definitely gives me a starting point for what I need to be looking up. What code will do this step?

trying something.

Just give it a shot! I have tried just giving it a shot and seeing if it works. I often find this frustrates me as the majority of the time it doesn't work. I usually get to this stage after I've moved through everything and I already have an idea of what it will look like. It can be handy in providing error messages so I can then see where I've gone wrong.

rubber ducky method.

The Rubber Ducky Method is when you talk something out as if you were talking to a rubber duck (or a pet or imaginary friend). I find the Rubber Ducky Method very useful. Often as I'm talking out loud I hear what I'm saying and realise it doesn't make sense, or it clicks something in me as I realised I've missed a step or misunderstood something. I have often used this (without knowing what it is) in my life when I've been trying to solve work related problems or help other people with problems.

reading error messages.

This is a tricky one because knowing what the error message is, requires problem solving in itself. I do find it useful as it allows me to look at where the problem is, identify the problem and fix it. At this stage though (and this is happening less and less) I often find that it will give me the error and I don't understand it because I don't know the code well enough to understand what the problem is. It'll tell me that something is undefined, but in my mind it is defined so it then results in more problem solving. I find it really useful for identifying the line where something isn't working and it allows me to move from there.

console.logging.

Console.logging is when instead of using return or call a function, you use console.log to see what the output actually is. I use this all the time. When I can see what it is that I'm actually outputting, I'm able to identify where I've gone wrong and I know what I want it to look like so I can work backwards from there. With HTML and CSS when you save and refresh your page you see the changes and what it looks like on your page. When writing Javascript (so far) I'm writing something but don't actually see what the visual output is. Using the console is a really great and useful way to see this.

googling.

I use Google all the time. Learning how to Google is another kettle of fish, and something I am definitely refining. Learning how to refine the results has definitely helped me as I know that by filtering my results to the previous year I am getting the most up-to-date information. Using key words is also helpful. Making sure I include the language that I am using is so important as there are so many different languages and platforms/apps that people use it's easy to look at something and get even more confused because it's not in the correct language.

asking peers for help.

This is something I am getting better at. It's good asking other people who are learning as they have also been through a similar problem and often trying to problem solve in a similar way so being able to have someone explain who is learning as well can be useful.

This is something I struggle with because of my own personal insecurities. I have done it a bit this week but I often feel like I am just not understanding. I am still trying to wrap my head around all the different concepts and what they mean and the different words for different things. I often find myself feeling embarrassed for not understanding something or feeling like I'm silly because of the way I'm doing something. This is purely my own insecurities and something I am working on getting past. improving process with reflection.

This is so important. Being able to go away and reflect on what I've just learn often allows me the opportunity to come back and finetune my code. Is there a better way of doing this? Can I get there in fewer steps? What did I find difficult? Why did I find it difficult? Reflecting on my learnings is very useful and is providing me with a lot of reassurance in the fact that compared to where I was a month ago, I have learnt a LOT of new information. I often start my day with looking back at what I did the previous say, having already solved the problems, and it allows me to a) feel proud of myself and b) work through each step again and see how I got there.
